## STUDENT PAGE

# The Life Stage Experiment

Open the Life Stage Calculator at **thefinancelab.co** (Free Tools, or
the Calculator link on the home page) on your laptop. It models five
stages of a working life. You can change how much is invested each
month, for how many years, and the return rate for every stage.

### Before you touch anything: two predictions

1. The default plan invests a total of about $529,000 across 44 years.
   Write down your guess: what is it worth at the end?

   My guess: $ ____________

2. Which stage do you think matters most to the final number: the
   teen years ($25 a month) or the peak earning years ($1,200 a month)?

   My guess: ____________

### Run 1: The default path

Press nothing. Just read the results and record:

- Total contributed: $ ____________
- Final balance: $ ____________
- Growth (balance minus contributed): $ ____________
- How far off was your guess? ____________

### Run 2: The late start

Set the first two stages (Teen/Student and First Job) to $0 a month.
Leave everything else alone.

- New final balance: $ ____________
- How much money did those two stages actually contribute in Run 1?
  (Hint: $25 a month for 4 years, then $200 a month for 5 years.)
  $ ____________
- How much did skipping them cost the final balance? $ ____________

### Run 3: The early bird

Reset, then double the Teen/Student stage to $50 a month and cut every
later stage in half.

- Total contributed: $ ____________
- Final balance: $ ____________
- Compare to Run 1: who invested more money, and who ended with more?

### Run 4: Your plan

Set every stage to numbers you actually believe about your own life.
Be honest, not optimistic.

- My total contributed: $ ____________
- My final balance: $ ____________

### Reflect

1. Which single change moved the final number the most? Why do you
   think that is?

2. The curve bends upward instead of climbing in a straight line.
   Explain why, in your own words.

3. Your Run 4 plan makes assumptions about your future. Name one that
   could turn out differently, and what you could do about it.

*The calculator's return rates are assumptions, not promises. All
investments involve risk, and past performance does not predict future
returns.*

## TEACHER NOTES

**The big idea.** Time in the market beats size of contribution.
Students discover it themselves by breaking the calculator, not by
being told.

**Where it fits.** A one-period companion to any investing lesson, or a
standalone for a sub day with laptops. Pairs naturally with The
Launchpad's Investing unit.

**Numbers for the board** (calculator defaults):

- Run 1: $529,200 contributed grows to about $2,115,000. Growth alone
  is about $1,586,000: the money made three times more than the person.
- Run 2: zeroing the first two stages skips only $13,200 of
  contributions but costs about $292,000 at the end. Every skipped
  dollar would have been worth about 22.
- Run 3: the early bird contributes about half as much as Run 1
  ($266,400) and still ends with over $1.1 million.

**Common misconceptions.**

- "The big contributions late matter most." Run 2 is the rebuttal:
  the small early dollars have the most time to compound.
- "Growth is linear." The reflection question about the bending curve
  is the check; listen for "the growth earns growth."
- "These numbers are guaranteed." They are not; the italic note on the
  student page says so, and it is worth saying out loud.

**Grading.** Grade the thinking, never the plan. Complete recorded runs
plus honest reflections is full credit; Run 4's actual numbers are the
student's own business.
